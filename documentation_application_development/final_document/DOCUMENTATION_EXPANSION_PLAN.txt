1. CURRENT REPORT WEAKNESSES
==================================================================

The following weaknesses have been identified through a side by side review of the current Main_Application_Development_Report.md, the supporting individual contribution document, and the lecturer-style QA notes already produced for this project. Each weakness is expressed in marker-friendly language and is supported with a specific reason.

1.1 Short sections

- Most numbered sections in the main report are between one and three short paragraphs. For example, Section 2 (Project Motivation), Section 3 (Problem Description), Section 5 (Application Overview), and the implementation sub-sections (10.1 to 10.11) are short summaries rather than full academic discussions.
- A high-mark London Met / ESOFT report normally develops each major section across multiple paragraphs that explain the academic problem, the chosen approach, the technical implementation, and the link to coursework requirements. The current report does not yet reach that depth.
- The reflection section (Section 18) is currently only one paragraph. A strong individual reflection should cover learning outcomes, challenges, decisions, and personal development across at least one full page.

1.2 Thin academic explanation

- The narrative is mostly descriptive ("the system does X") rather than analytical ("the system does X because of requirement Y, supported by source file Z, addressing risk W").
- Important academic concepts are mentioned but not explained, including separation of concerns, the MVC pattern, the role of view models, dependency injection, the Identity pipeline, EF Core change tracking, anti-forgery protection, and the rationale for role-based access control.
- The report does not currently include a research and background section that links the chosen technologies to academic literature, official documentation, or established design principles. Section 7 (Research and Background) exists but is only one paragraph.
- There is no explicit mapping between the case-study problem and software-engineering principles such as cohesion, coupling, single responsibility, layered architecture, and defence in depth.

1.3 Lack of lecturer-style marking alignment

- The current report includes a coursework task mapping (Table 7) but it is high level. It does not give per-criterion marking commentary that explicitly states what evidence the marker should look at and where it lives.
- A lecturer-style alignment is already drafted separately in LECTURER_MARKING_ALIGNMENT_REPORT.txt, but the main report itself does not include the same per-item marking discussion in academic prose.
- Items such as "Programming style", "Validation and exception handling", and "Interface design and usability" do not yet have their own focused sections inside the body of the report. They are only mentioned in tables.

1.4 Need more screenshots and evidence

- The main report references Figures 7 to 19 but no actual screenshot files are present in documentation_application_development/screenshots/ yet.
- The Evidence Checklist confirms that screenshot capture is still pending across login, registration, dashboards, course management, enrolment, assignment, grading, reporting, communication, validation, database, repository, and packaged build evidence.
- Diagrams are stored as Mermaid sources in documentation_application_development/diagrams/ but exported PNG or PDF images for the report have not been created and inserted yet.
- There is no captured database evidence (table view, migration list export, or backup view) and no captured build evidence beyond a textual record of the build result.

1.5 Need deeper implementation explanation

- Section 10 (Implementation) lists each module in one short paragraph. It does not explain the controller actions, route templates, authorisation attributes, validation rules, view-model binding, EF Core query patterns, error responses, or the relationship between controller, service, and database for each feature.
- The relationship between Razor views, view models, layout, partials, and Tag Helpers is not explained in detail.
- The role of supporting infrastructure such as audit logging, rate limiting, file store services, security headers, CSV writer, and PDF report builder is mentioned but not explained in technical depth.
- The implementation discussion does not include short, illustrative source-code snippets in fenced blocks, which are normally expected in a technical report at this level.

1.6 Need clearer logical solution section

- Section 12 (Logical Solution Explanation) is a single paragraph. It does not yet show the end-to-end logical flow from user request to controller action to service to EF Core to database and back to view.
- A high-mark logical solution should explain how each requirement from the case study is mapped to a specific MVC structure (controllers, models, view models, views, infrastructure) and to a specific data flow (request, validation, business rules, persistence, response, audit).
- The current section does not yet include a request-handling pipeline narrative, a transaction-style narrative for grading or enrolment, or a summary of cross-cutting concerns.

1.7 Need stronger reflection

- Section 18 (Own Reflection) is short and general. It does not yet describe specific decisions taken during implementation, alternatives considered, problems encountered, lessons learned, soft-skill development, time management, and the impact of the approved individual submission on planning and effort.
- A high-mark reflection at London Met / ESOFT level normally covers technical learning, professional learning, ethical considerations (data protection, academic integrity), and a forward-looking statement about the author's continued development.

1.8 Need stronger user manual

- The user manual (User_Manual_Application_Development.md) covers installation, login, role guides, and troubleshooting at a basic level. The descriptions are adequate but lack the screenshot-walkthrough detail expected in a marked installation and user manual.
- Pre-requisites are listed but not justified. Database options are listed but not visually illustrated. Each role-by-role workflow is described in bullet points rather than step-by-step procedures with figure references and expected outcomes.
- The viva demonstration notes section is short and could be expanded into a full demonstration script that links each role to a planned screen sequence.

1.9 Need better appendices

- Appendices A to F are listed but most of them only have a heading and no detailed content yet.
- A high-mark report normally includes:
  * Appendix A: selected source-code listings with file path and line range references.
  * Appendix B: full set of inserted screenshots with figure captions.
  * Appendix C: editable diagram sources and exported diagram images.
  * Appendix D: individual approval evidence and planning timeline.
  * Appendix E: source repository link, packaged build link, and database evidence.
  * Appendix F: full user manual either embedded or referenced.
  * Additional appendices for test logs, audit log samples, validation messages, and reference URLs.

1.10 Other secondary weaknesses

- The List of Figures and List of Tables exist but the figures and tables they reference are not yet finalised inside the body of the report at academic depth.
- The references list contains placeholders such as [official Microsoft documentation link]. Final Harvard-style citations have not yet been written.
- The abbreviations list is short and could be extended to cover EF Core, Identity, RBAC, HSTS, CSRF, OWASP, and CDN, all of which are used in the discussion.
- The abstract is brief and does not yet summarise the methodology, findings, and conclusion in the academic abstract style normally expected.

Style requirements applied throughout:

- London Met / ESOFT academic tone.
- UK English (organise, behaviour, programme where relevant, although "program" is retained when referring to executable software).

5. INDIVIDUAL SUBMISSION WORDING PLAN
==================================================================

This section explains how the report will remain a clear individual approved submission while honestly acknowledging that the coursework was originally group based. The wording must avoid blame, conflict, group contribution tables, group task allocation, and any negative comment about other students.

5.1 Core wording principles

- The author refers to themselves consistently as "the author" or in first person ("I") in reflective sections, with one consistent voice across the whole report.
- The phrase "approved individual submission" is used in the title page, declaration, acknowledgement, abstract (briefly), introduction, contribution chapter, and conclusion.
- The report does not list group members, does not split tasks between students, and does not include comparison tables of who did what.
- The report does not blame, criticise, or describe disagreements with any other student.
- The report does not state that other students failed to contribute. It also does not state that the author "took over" anyone's work. It simply states that the coursework was approved to be completed individually.

5.2 Approved individual submission boilerplate

A neutral wording block will be reused, in slightly varied forms, in the title page, declaration, acknowledgement, introduction, contribution chapter, and conclusion. Suggested base form:

"This coursework was originally set as group work. Following approval from the lecturer / module team, the author has completed the implementation, testing, documentation, and final preparation as an individual submission. The full report and supporting evidence reflect the author's own work."

Variations should be slightly rephrased so that the same exact sentence does not appear in every section, but the meaning stays neutral and honest.

5.3 Section-by-section wording approach

- Title page: a single line stating "Submission Type: Approved individual submission".
- Declaration: a short paragraph confirming personal authorship and approved individual completion.
- Acknowledgement: thanks the lecturer and module team for guidance and approval, without naming or blaming any other student.
- Abstract: one short clause that the report represents an approved individual submission. No further detail.
- Chapter 1 (Introduction): a short paragraph in Section 1.3 explaining the approved individual submission context in neutral, factual terms.
- Chapter 8 (Implementation), Chapter 15 (Testing), Chapter 16 (Evaluation): the work is described as the author's own work. No "we" usage.
- Chapter 19 (Reflection): the author may discuss the experience of preparing for an individual completion, including time management, scope control, and personal learning, without describing any conflict with other students.
- Chapter 21 (Conclusion): closes with a brief restatement that this submission is the author's own work under the approved individual route.

5.4 Wording to use

Acceptable phrases:

- "approved individual submission"
- "the author"
- "the author's own implementation"
- "the author completed the implementation, testing, and documentation"
- "following approval from the lecturer / module team"
- "this report has been prepared as an individual submission"
- "individual approved completion route"
- "personal authorship is confirmed by the author"

5.5 Wording to avoid

The following types of wording must not appear anywhere in the report:

- Names of other students.
- Statements about who did or did not contribute to the original group.
- Comparison tables of group member contributions.
- Group task allocation tables.
- Communication-record tables or screenshots of disputes.
- Phrases that imply blame, such as "other members did not contribute", "I had to do everything", or "the group failed".
- Phrases that imply emotional conflict, such as "unfair", "let down", or "abandoned".
- Phrases that imply unauthorised individual completion, such as "I decided on my own to submit alone".
- Use of "we" or "the team" when describing the actual implementation work that has been verified as the author's own.

5.6 Voice consistency

- First person singular ("I") is allowed in the reflection chapter and where the author describes personal learning.
- "The author" is preferred in the body chapters (Implementation, Architecture, Database, Testing, Evaluation) so that the report reads as a neutral technical report.
- "We" is not used for implementation work. It may only appear inside cited material from external references where the original source uses it.

5.7 Final wording check before submission

Before exporting the final PDF, perform a wording sweep that confirms:

- "approved individual submission" appears in the title page, declaration, introduction, contribution chapter, and conclusion.
- No other student is named, blamed, compared, or described.
- No group contribution table, work-splitting table, or communication record is included.
- No emotional or accusatory language is present.
- The voice is consistent (first person in reflection, third-person "the author" elsewhere).
- The report avoids em dashes and strange symbols and uses UK English spelling throughout.
